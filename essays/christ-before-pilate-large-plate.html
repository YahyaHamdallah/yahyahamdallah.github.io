patior (verb) - to suffer and endure

During the height of the Thirty Years War, how does one showcase the efforts and turbulence of religious and political tensions within the Holy Roman Empire? Deep divisions between Catholics, Lutherans, and Calvinists spilled beyond a religious civil war, rather it spiraled and highlighted the efforts of European dominance.

Rembrandt, living in the aftermath of the Protestant Reformation in the Netherlands, where Protestant nobles revolted against the efforts to impose religious uniformity, uses The Passion of Christ to pose a deeper question:

but who is in a position to decree justice or religious freedom?

Showcasing Pilate who served as a pivotal role in the trial of Jesus in the new testament, Rembrandt is showing us the juxtaposition between human authority, justice, and morality. While power was contested by rulers, it was the ordinary faithful who suffered and endured. Left to navigate and question, who is this fight really for?

In this scene, the ordinary dissolves into the background. Faint and wiped away. Barely visible in the masses. The crowd appears as if a storm of mass hysteria consumes them. Those around Pilate are seen as defined figures of higher rank. Yet only three individuals are actually looking at Pilate, highlighting this idea of hierarchy and the breakdown of civility and order. It is almost a theatrical stage where power is center stage, and the common people dissolve into an indistinct sea.

Each time I return to the piece, it feels like a new encounter. Rembrandt's technique of wiping the plate while etching creates an atmosphere of fogginess, like a faint memory or dream. Pilate's stark brightness clearly pulls our attention with just a few striking lines. While the masses are lost in the haze of suffering, those who surround Pilate are clearly defined, showcasing how authority is sharp and visible. Rembrandt captures the tension between authority's clarity and the masses confusion.
