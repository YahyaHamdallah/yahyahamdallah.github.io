Formal Analysis of Caspar David Friedrich

The formal elements in Caspar David Friedrich’s piece, The Sea of Ice (1824), also called The Wreck of the Hope, collectively and deliberately present the onlooker with a dynamic piece that moves their eye across the terrain. The formal organization of the rock base in the foreground immediately captures the eye with its warmer tone and carries the viewer along the base of the piece, pushing their eye horizontally across the piece from left to right. Friedrich steers the viewer’s eye across the entire base, moving back and forth, noting the immense detail in the painting along the way when viewed up close.

Friedrich steers the viewer’s eye across the entire base.

The observer resumes moving across the piece until they reach a rock facing the sky in the lower right. The sharp line results in the viewer continuing upwards, where they reach another rock pointing in the opposing direction. The implied line pushes the viewer’s eye to the middle of the piece and further back into space. The viewer continues to move horizontally across the piece where they reach a section where the ice is lifted, yielding further examination in that area. This slight rise creates an even plane that does not disrupt horizontal movement across the piece, causing the viewer’s eye to not jump directly into the background. Under the broken ice is a sunken ship that has slightly started to rise to the surface.

The implied line pushes the viewer’s eye further back into space.

Friedrich’s use of tone is key to the success of creating a sense of environment and a rescission into the background. To bring the viewers back to the painting and not get lost off the canvas, Friedrich’s use of cooler tones in the background brings the viewer’s eyes toward that direction, yet, the use of warmer tones draws the audience back to the foreground. Separating the foreground, atmosphere, and background while connecting them all through movement and repetition. With the ship being in a cooler tone than the rocks but having a darker blue compared to the atmosphere and red detailing, it connects back to the rigid rocks allowing the viewer to continue in the horizontal movement to the ship’s left. As well, the direction of the ice pushes the viewer back to the left.

Movement and repetition connect the foreground, atmosphere, and background.

The viewer continues up the formation, where Friedrich grabs the viewer’s attention by having four repeating structures in the background pointing in the same general direction off the page. This causes the viewer to go back to moving in a horizontal direction, looking for more across the entire plane. Friedrich uses tone to aid the viewer’s eye in traveling into the background and reducing the form to shapes rather than the immense detail in the foreground, adding to the idea that these formations are receding into space. Creating a dynamic piece allows the audience to bring their eyes back to the entire whilst making it appear that there is more to see.
